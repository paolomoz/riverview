Vandra Linder Patient Story

November 10, 2025

Patient Story

About 2022 during a walk in the neighborhood I tore my left knee meniscus on an uneven portion of the pavement. I did nothing at that time other than icing it, had an ortho surgeon look at it, and received a cortisone shot. I have always had slight knocked knees but by 2024 due to compensating for the left knee, my right knee started to cave in, creating a valgus knee. At 70+ years, and after my son, an ortho surgical tech, urged me to have knee replacement surgery.  I agreed, as I had just finished a strenuous European vacation with lots of stairs and uneven walkways. At his suggestion, I contacted Dr. Malin. I had my right knee in December 2024, and the left knee in January 2025. Probably due to overcompensation, the right patella needed to be resurfaced in the spring 2024.

The staff, physician assistant and Dr. Malin, were all very friendly, professional, courteous, warm and caring. Dr. Malin, Dr. Jen Burch PA and Elizabeth are exceptional, both with concern about my improvement and assistance on a pending billing issue.

Overall, the quality of the environment and care at the hospital, post-op, was excellent.

The recovery period was not as painful or uncomfortable as I was up and about without assistance within two days. As I am about a year out, my knees are aligned, and I am walking comfortably and well. There is some stiffness in both, noticeable if I miss stretching, but that will pass in time. I am looking forward to more European vacations, hiking, and being active again with my Grandchildren. 

About Knee Replacement at Riverview Health

Knee arthroplasty — the procedure Vandra underwent on both knees — swaps a damaged knee joint for a prosthetic one so the lower leg can bend and move freely again. Surgeons generally recommend it when stiffness interferes with daily routines, when X-rays reveal serious loss of cartilage and bone, when discomfort continues even at rest, when the joint has become deformed, or after injections and therapy have failed to bring lasting relief.

Depending on the extent of the damage, the operation may replace the whole joint or just one side of it, and candidates can ask whether a minimally invasive approach might mean a quicker rebound. Most people feel close to normal within 8-12 weeks, though rebuilding full strength can continue for up to a year.
